1. Cybersecurity

 

Q. What does it do?

 

Cybersecurity is a means to protect anything that is connected to a network. Being connected to a network involves hardware such as laptops, mobiles, desktops and servers. Even software for communication and work applications. Everyone is vulnerable to a cyber-attack. Businesses on the other hand are more likely to be targeted over an individual. The common threats are spyware, Trojans, virus, phishing and network intrusion. Deep learning is a state of the art new technology for cybersecurity. In this area of AI it incorporates machine learning, where the machines are able to learn from their experience and attain skills without human interaction. It is an algorithm that performs a task over and over again and each time it does there are changes to the outcome. 

 

To make this development possible, throughout the past few years technology has changed dramatically. We are at the point where virtual assistants are available now. Virtual assistants can be Siri, Alexa and Cortana. Service providers tend to incorporate deep learning to understand speech and the language that humans use when interacting with these virtual assistants. Websites back then did not have the option for the users to interact with a bot to provide customer service or get assistant. Whereas now majority of websites have chatbots and service bots available to assist users with their enquiry. By having these bots available for companies, it makes it easier for them to deal with customer assistance and the bots are able to respond in a helpful way due to the increase in auditory and text questions.

 

With deep learning now it is able to translate between languages which is very convenient for travellers and people in business industry. The machine is able to translate majority of text typed or inserted however the meaning could vary depending on the context and how it is used. For basic use it is perfectly fine but if one wishes to use it for further gains it is not recommended. Deep learning is also used for facial recognition, not just for security purposes such as a two factor authentication.

 

In the next 3 years or in the near future with deep learning we may be able to have an advanced payment option, which does not involve the user having to pay with cash, credit card or using their watch. Instead people will be able to pay with facial recognition, where the machines are able to determine the person based on their facial features such as eye colour, nose, hairstyle, beard, etc. However the current challenge as of now is to get facial recognition to identify people even if they have changes to their appearances. Such as with Apple devices, they incorporate facial recognition as one of their security features to unlock devices or either by entering a passcode. 

 

Could potentially have driverless cars and delivery trucks, where the machine learns through deep learning algorithms. With more data they are able to absorb they better the result will be. With a driverless car it could be an uber. It has the similar features to a virtual assistant where it is able to understand what the users says and will execute the command. An example could be the car is driving and approaches a roundabout, it will be know when to stop and when to drive depending on the situation if there are any other vehicles in the vicinity, it would do the same thing that a regular drive would do. With this implemented self-driving cars will be able to adapt to road change situations and conditions along with also learning new road situations. To make this possible, the vehicles will require real time visual and sensor data.

3. Autonomous Vehicles

 

Q. What does It do?

 

Autonomous vehicles are self-driving vehicles which drives from a starting point to a destination on auto pilot. These vehicles are capable of doing such things due to the various technology and sensors it contains. The first autonomous vehicle was the Stanford cart from the 1980s to the 1960s which was used as a lunar rover. Now the autonomous vehicle company ‘Waymo’ is the current leading state of the art for this technology. Waymo is designed to function without a driver, their autonomous vehicles “operates a commercial self-driving taxi”(Fingas, 2018), users can download the Waymo mobile app similar to uber to then ask for an autonomous vehicle which is available 24/7, giving the users a driverless experience. The autonomous vehicle will arrive at the users starting point where they board the vehicle which it will then drive to their destination on auto pilot.

 

However, Waymo’s self-driving taxis is currently exclusive to Phoenix, Arizona and its services are limited to users who took part in Waymo test programs. This limitation is due to Waymo starting cautiously hoping to avoid collisions and easing the community into a driverless world (Fingas, 2018). Next, Waymo will slowly progress by continuing to test its autonomous vehicles in different environments and landscapes. So far, the company has been giving permission to test its vehicles completely driverless in California but unfortunately have not been permitted to test elsewhere. This is due to the public not having complete trust with driverless taxis as well as these autonomous vehicles not having enough training data. The perceived way to train an autonomous vehicle is to provide it with billions of hours of real driving and utilise this to teach the vehicle good driving behaviour (Piper, 2020). However, gaining this kind of data which is essential for the development of autonomous vehicles is hard as it is expensive. Also, encountering other learning experiences such car accidents or coming across obstacles such as debris on the road is very rare, making the progression of autonomous vehicles slow (Piper, 2020). 

 

Waymo utilises various technologies and sensors to make the ideal autonomous vehicle. Waymo uses a combination of sensors and systems such as LiDar system, Radar system, Vision system and supplemental sensors. By combining these technologies Waymo gives its vehicles the ability to see 360-degrees at the maximum distance of 300m (Andrew J, 2020), and in some circumstances see stop signs from as far as 500m away. The LiDar system is placed on top of the vehicle for 360-degree view while the other two LiDar systems are placed at the front of the car for perimeter vision and on the side for peripheral vision. The LiDar sends out a pulse of light collecting data of up to 4 different objects in the laser beams line of sight (Andrew J, 2020). This helps give a more detailed view of the environment. The vision system is a high-resolution camera which is primarily used for long range in both daylight and nightlight. This camera detects various different colours to help identify traffic lights as well as construction and road signs (Gibbs, 2020). The radar system utilises wavelengths, this radar helps identify movements in the proximity of the vehicle to avoid accidents (Gibbs, 2020). Lastly the Waymo autonomous vehicle uses a supplemental sensor, this is an audio detection sensor which is used as extra support to identify the sounds of emergency vehicle sirens which are very distant. The vehicle can use this information to prepare for when the emergency services near and act accordingly, such as pulling over or making way. Also, lastly the Waymo vehicle uses a GPS as a supplement to gain a better understanding of the location it currently resides so it is able to accurately understand the environment and landscape that it is going to be travelling in (Gibbs, 2020).

4. Robots

 

Q. What does it do? 

 

Robots are machines which are programmed to complete complex tasks or tasks and action which are traditionally completed by humans. Robots are used in many industries to perform repetitive tasks and also used to work in environments hazardous to people such as disarming bombs and putting out fires. The ASIMO robot which stands for, Advanced Step In Innovative Ability, is the state of the art of this technology with its humanoid appearance and its ability to walk and climb up stairs (Staple, 2019). The ASIMO robot is the result of twenty years’ worth of robot research collected by Honda. 

 

ASIMO is currently capable of executing human actions such as running and walking on flat and uneven landscapes as well as grabbing objects with ease. The robot’s ability to walk and run has advanced to the extent where it is able to change its direction smoothly while moving. ASIMO has the ability to interpret simple voice commands and respond, it has learnt responses as simple as responding to its name when it is called. It is also capable of other abilities such as recognising objects, gestures, sounds and faces, this helps ASIMO interact with humans and act accordingly (Jason, 2011).

 

Although ASIMO is capable of executing advanced actions there is still improvements to be made. Production, study and improvement of ASIMO was discontinued in 2018 but the advanced robot was still developing on certain movements such as jogging and jumping and recovering from sudden forces such as being shoved or pulled. Honda was working on this by implementing a new strategy where the robot takes longer and faster steps to recover from the forceful shoves (Ackerman, 2018). The focus of this is to help the robot keep balance, this will be achieved by providing ASIMO with information on how the human recovers from incidents. The development of ASIMO has been halted due to Honda wanting to use ASIMO’s technology for more practical research and to focus on nursing and road transport. In saying this it is not sure if the advanced humanoid robot will be making a comeback, but its technology has set the margin for robots. 

 

ASIMO’s design was inspired by the research of insect and mammal legs as well as the study of movement by paraplegic people with prosthetic legs. This study was used to accurately understand how the leg functions as well as the physiology. The robot contains many sensors and advanced technology to for it to function. ASIMO has the two sensors gyroscope sensor and speed sensor placed on its body. These sensors are responsible of keeping track of the position the body is in as well as the speed it travels in. These sensors perform to help keep the equilibrium of the robot like the inner ear of a human (Obringer, 2018). The robot has “floor surface sensors in its feet and six ultrasonic sensors in its midsection” (Obringer, 2018), these sensors make it possible for ASIMO to detect objects around itself and interact with its surrounding environment. The control of the posture and the reinforcement of the robot’s balance is handled by floor reaction control, target ZMP control and Foot-planting location control. The floor reaction control is focused on the feet and absorbs the unevenness of the landscape it walks on helping ASIMO to keep balance. The Target ZMP control is control of counterbalancing a fall when ASIMO is on the verge of falling over, this is achieved by ASIMO speeding up and moving its body in the opposite direction to stop a fall. The last bit of control technology ASIMO contains is Foot-planting location control. This control is activated when Target ZMP control is in use, this control adjusts the stride and step ASIMO takes to match the speed in steps and posture the robot has to be in to impend a fall.
