IT Work  

 

 

  The IT industry is exponentially expanding and remains a hero of the Fourth Revolution. Information technology is often the foundation of other industries to help them evolve. This is largely due to the number of experts who have worked to constantly develop IT related technologies during the early stages of industry.  

 

 

Bill Gates

  Bill Gates, co-founder of Microsoft, who is renowned as the world's largest software company, played an important role in providing technology to the public. Since he was young, he had a dream of a computer with one in every desk and one in every house, and he made a lot of effort to achieve that dream. He developed a computer game called ‘tic-tac-toc’ with his natural talent, which led to his first meeting with Paul Allen, another IT expert who co-founder of Microsoft. Since then, he developed the world's first commercial prefabricated personal computer, MITS Altair 8800, in 1975, which led to the creation of Microsoft. Since then, he has spent a lot of time developing software to build on-PC systems, which eventually led to the success of developing the "Windows" computer operating system, which is still popular today, to help create a pleasant PC environment. Bill Gates had a hard time because 90 percent of the users of the "Windows" OS didn't pay. He didn't budge from his own argument that he should pay a fair fee and use it, and in the end he won. 

 

 

Steve Jobs

  If Bill Gates built an OS called Windows, Steve Jobs successfully built a Mac OS that was different from Windows. He is also known for his pioneering work in personal computing and product design. In the past, Jobs, who was passionate about computing, met with Apple co-founder Steve Wozniak at HP's summer workplace, and they were active in places like computer club meetings, as well as engineers at Atari, a popular video game maker. Seeing Wozniak's self-designed computer, Jobs convinced his neighbors to sell the product, and he took this opportunity to set up Apple Computer and enter the personal computer business. The initial plan was to sell only printed circuit boards, but the two had great success selling a fully assembled computer, 'McKintoshi'. But Jobs also had a crisis. Apple's market share fell sharply as Apple struggled to launch a new operating system. At this time, Jobs worked hard to develop not only computers but also innovative things such as iPhones, iPads, iMacs, and iPods, which made Apple's position even stronger. 

 

  

 

Tim Berners-Lee

  Since computers and smartphones have become available, we don't go to the library to look for books when we are curious, but rather do 'search' using computers and smartphones. When I click, the information I'm looking for comes up in no time. We can easily find information about everything that happens in the global community without going through the books one by one or going far away. It's entirely thanks to the World Wide Web that this is possible. This is a system built by Tim Berners Lee, a British computer scientist. While working at the European Institute of Particle Physics (CERN) in Switzerland, he had a hard time exchanging information with employees. That's because more than 10,000 employees were far apart, and they were using different hardware and software. He developed 'Enquir', a new system for exchanging information with employees, which was the basis of the World Wide Web, unifying how to find various kinds of information, collecting and connecting scattered information. 

 

  

 

Michael Dell 

  Mike Dell is the founder and CEO of Dell, one of the world's leading computer vendors for personal computers. After entering college, he entered the IT world by launching an informal business of assembling and selling upgrade kits for personal computers. He founded the company named ‘Dell’ to start his own business, judging that there are tremendous benefits to potential cost savings if a PC manufacturer sells products directly to customers without going through a retailer. He hired and communicated with other IT professionals as well as experienced managers who could serve as personal mentors. But he had a hard time, too. After 4.1 million recalls and complaints, there have been constant disputes with shareholders, and he eventually turned the company over to another company. 

 

  

 

James Gosling

  James Gosling, often called "Dr.Java," is a Canadian computer scientist who has made his name known as the founder and chief designer of Java programming languages. He got the idea for a Java VM by converting a Perq Q-Code into a VAX assembly, emulating hardware, and writing a program to implant software in PERQ. He was successful in conclusion, allowing Java to create powerful code that runs on a variety of devices, including mobile phones, PCs, and mainframes. Thanks to this achievement, in 2002, the ACM Software System Award which is given to the person who developed the powerful software system was awarded to him.
